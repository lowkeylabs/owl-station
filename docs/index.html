OWL-ROOST

Retirement Options and Outcomes Studies Tool

ROOST (Retirement Options and Outcomes Studies Tool) evaluates retirement decision policies—not just static plans—by comparing how agent-controlled actions perform under uncertainty when those decisions are revisited annually.

ROOST is designed to answer questions like:

“Given the uncertainty I face, how flexible are my retirement decisions—and which decision policies are most robust?”

It does this by organizing retirement analysis around a small number of clear, orthogonal concepts.

Core Concepts

ROOST introduces six key concepts in extending the work of OWL.

  • Decision options define what question is being asked
  • Choice templates define policy alternatives
  • Cases define initial conditions and constraints
  • Runs define a fixed decision policy applied to a case
  • Trials define individual realizations of uncertainty
  • Results provide evidence for comparison and insight

Cases and runs are shared between OWL and ROOST. They are represented by exactly the same files and outputs.

Decision Options

A decision option represents a focused retirement question—something the agent can choose and re-choose over time.

Examples:

  • Roth conversion strategy
  • Spending policy
  • Social Security claiming strategy
  • Retirement timing
  • Asset allocation policy

Each decision option defines a policy dimension to be explored, not a single action.

Choice Templates

A choice template is a concrete decision policy within a decision option.
It defines a rule for choosing an action each year, conditional on the current state.

Examples:

  • Roth options:
    • No Roth conversions
    • Fixed annual conversion amounts
    • Fill to a target marginal tax bracket
    • Let OWL optimize Roth conversions
  • Spending options:
    • Fixed real spending
    • Guardrail spending
    • Utility-maximizing spending
    • “Smile” spending profile

Choice templates are reusable, comparable, and represent different ways to answer the same underlying decision question.

Cases

A case defines the initial state and constraints of a retirement scenario.

A case is specified by:

  • A TOML plan file
  • A referenced Household Financial Profile (HFP) file

Cases encode exogenous assumptions that the agent does not directly control, including:

  • Longevity assumptions
  • Rates of return and inflation assumptions
  • Tax rules and policy parameters
  • Household structure, income sources, and contribution rules

These values are treated as given for a case and are held fixed when comparing decision policies.

Cases are sources of truth and are independent of computed outcomes.

Uncertainty

Uncertainty represents aspects of the world that affect outcomes but are not controlled by the agent.

In ROOST, uncertainty is treated as exogenous:

  • Longevity outcomes
  • Market return paths
  • Inflation paths (future implementation)
  • Policy and tax environments (future implementation)

Decision policies are evaluated conditional on uncertainty, allowing ROOST to explore sensitivity and robustness without conflating choices with outcomes.

This separation allows ROOST to answer questions such as:

  • How sensitive is a decision policy to different longevity outcomes?
  • Which policies remain robust across pessimistic and optimistic return environments?

The key conceptual distinction is simple:

Decisions are chosen by the agent; uncertainty is not.

Runs

A run represents a fixed decision policy instance applied to a single case.

Conceptually:

Case × Choice Template → Run

A run answers the question:

“What decision policy are we studying?”

All agent-controlled decisions are fixed at the run level.

Trials

A trial represents a single realization of uncertainty for a given run.

Conceptually:

Run × Uncertainty Realization → Trial

Across trials:

  • The decision policy is fixed
  • Only random or uncertain elements differ

Trials allow ROOST to distinguish decision quality from luck, and to evaluate how the same policy performs across many plausible futures.

Runs vs Trials

Runs and trials serve different but complementary roles:

  • A run defines intent — the policy being evaluated
  • A trial defines experience — what happened under a particular realization of uncertainty

Multiple trials may be generated for a single run to assess robustness, tail risk, and sensitivity to uncertainty.

Results

Results are the artifacts produced by OWL and ROOST for runs and trials and form the basis for evaluation and comparison.

Examples include:

  • Objective values (e.g., maximum sustainable spending, bequest)
  • Effective (post-override) TOML and HFP artifacts
  • Summary and metrics JSON files
  • Time-series projections and distributions
  • Comparative tables and visualizations

Results are fully reproducible from the effective TOML and HFP combined with the recorded uncertainty realization.

How the Pieces Fit Together

  • Decision options define what question is being asked
  • Choice templates define policy alternatives
  • Cases define initial conditions and constraints
  • Runs define a fixed policy applied to a case
  • Trials define realizations of uncertainty
  • Results provide evidence for comparison and insight

In short:

Decision options define the questions;
choice templates define the policies;
cases define the assumptions;
runs define the fixed policy instances;
trials define realized uncertainty;
results define the evidence.

Design Philosophy

ROOST treats retirement planning as a sequential decision problem:

  • Decisions are agent-controlled
  • They are revisited annually
  • Outcomes unfold under uncertainty
  • Policies are evaluated across many plausible futures for robustness, not just optimality

Rather than asking “What is the single optimal plan?”, ROOST helps answer:

“Which decision policies perform well across many plausible futures—and how much flexibility do I really have?”

Relation to OWL-Planner

OWL-Planner

OWL-Planner (Optimal Wealth Laboratory) is a retirement planning and optimization framework that computes optimal retirement strategies over a full planning horizon, subject to detailed tax rules, account constraints, longevity assumptions, and return scenarios.

Given a fully specified input case—defined by a TOML plan file and a referenced Household Financial Profile (HFP)—OWL solves for optimal actions (such as spending levels, asset allocation, and Roth conversions) under the assumed conditions.

OWL answers questions of the form:

“Given these assumptions and constraints, what strategy is optimal over the planning horizon?”

How ROOST Extends OWL

ROOST does not replace OWL. Instead, ROOST extends OWL by systematizing how retirement decisions are explored, compared, and interpreted under uncertainty.

In particular, ROOST introduces a structured framework for:

  1. Separating agent-controlled decisions from uncertainty
  2. Organizing analysis around decision policies rather than single optimized plans
  3. Evaluating robustness across many uncertainty realizations
  4. Systematically comparing alternative policies

When to Use OWL vs ROOST

OWL and ROOST are complementary tools, designed to answer different—but closely related—questions.

Use OWL-Planner when you want to:

  • Solve a single, well-defined retirement case
  • Compute an optimal strategy under fixed assumptions
  • Explore detailed tax, account, and timing constraints

Use ROOST when you want to:

  • Compare decision policies, not just one plan
  • Explore robustness and flexibility under uncertainty
  • Systematically evaluate families of strategies
  • Understand sensitivity to first-year decisions

In One Sentence

Use OWL to compute optimal strategies for a given case;
use ROOST to compare and understand decision policies across uncertainty.
